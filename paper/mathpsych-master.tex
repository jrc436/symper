\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates human ability to innately perceive multiple
different features of visual symmetry. While most work has focused on
simple bilateral reflection symmetry, this study shows that humans are
sensitive to other types of symmetry as well. Wallpaper images out of
17 wallpaper groups were used to identify sensitivity resulting from
differences in symmetry features between the groups. 
106 subjects identified 272 image pairs matched for symmetry groups again distractors.  
Evidence was found that rotation and reflection are not sufficient to
explain perception of symmetry, challenging some prevailing notions.
